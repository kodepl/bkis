## Introduction: The Colourless Fuel with a Green Future

Among the myriad solutions proposed for our climate crisis, one stands out for its elegant simplicity and transformative potential: green hydrogen. This remarkable energy carrier—produced using renewable electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen—promises to decarbonise sectors that have stubbornly resisted other clean technologies. Heavy industry, long-haul aviation, shipping, and seasonal energy storage may all find their salvation in this lightest of elements.

Industry currently produces approximately seventy million tonnes of hydrogen annually, but virtually all is "grey hydrogen" manufactured from natural gas—emitting roughly ten tonnes of carbon dioxide per tonne of hydrogen. Green hydrogen reimagines this pathway by powering electrolysis with renewable sources, creating a fuel whose only by-product is water. This article examines why it may prove indispensable to achieving net-zero emissions.

## Understanding Hydrogen Colour Codes

### The Spectrum of Hydrogen Production

Hydrogen is conventionally distinguished by production colour. Grey hydrogen from natural gas dominates but carries substantial emissions. Blue hydrogen incorporates carbon capture but remains fossil-fuel dependent. Green hydrogen—produced through renewable-powered electrolysis—represents the gold standard with zero direct emissions. Pink hydrogen uses nuclear power, while turquoise hydrogen employs methane pyrolysis.

## The Science of Electrolysis

### How Green Hydrogen Is Produced

Electrolysis uses a direct current to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. The process requires approximately fifty to fifty-five kilowatt-hours per kilogram of hydrogen at current efficiencies—the primary economic challenge of green hydrogen production.

### Electrolyser Technologies

Three main electrolyser technologies compete: alkaline electrolysis (established, low cost, but slower response), PEM electrolysis (higher efficiency, compact, but requires precious metal catalysts), and solid oxide cells (highest efficiency at very high temperatures, still in development).

## Applications and Demand Sectors

### Industrial Decarbonisation

Industry accounts for one-third of global emissions, with many processes poorly suited to direct electrification. Green hydrogen can replace coke in steel production, replace grey hydrogen in ammonia and fertiliser synthesis, and eliminate emissions from petroleum refining.

### Transport Applications

In aviation, hydrogen offers superior energy density for long-haul flights, with Airbus targeting hydrogen-powered aircraft by 2035. Shipping explores hydrogen-derived ammonia and methanol as alternatives to heavy fuel oil. For heavy road transport, fuel cell vehicles offer advantages in range and refuelling speed.

### Energy Storage and Grid Balancing

Green hydrogen offers a solution for long-duration energy storage—powering electrolysis during surplus generation, storing hydrogen in caverns or pipelines, and regenerating electricity when needed. Unlike batteries, hydrogen scales economically for seasonal storage. Power-to-X applications can convert stored hydrogen into synthetic fuels compatible with existing infrastructure.

## The Economic Landscape

### Cost Trajectories and Competitiveness

Green hydrogen currently costs approximately £4–6 per kilogram compared to £1–2 for grey hydrogen. However, costs are falling through declining renewable electricity prices, electrolyser scale-up, and improved efficiency. Cost parity could be achieved by 2030 in favourable locations.

### Policy Support and Strategic Investments

Governments worldwide are deploying substantial support. The EU targets twenty million tonnes by 2030. The UK aims for ten gigawatts of production capacity. The US Inflation Reduction Act offers tax credits up to $3 per kilogram. Middle Eastern and North African countries with abundant solar resources are positioning themselves as future exporters.

### Infrastructure Requirements

Scaling requires massive infrastructure investment in electrolyser manufacturing, adapted pipeline networks, geological storage facilities, refuelling stations, and port infrastructure for international trade.

## Challenges and Considerations

### Efficiency and Energy Economics

The round-trip efficiency of power-to-hydrogen-to-power is approximately forty percent, compared to eighty-five percent for batteries. This inefficiency means hydrogen should be reserved for hard-to-abate sectors where direct electrification is impractical.

### Water Consumption

Electrolysis requires approximately nine litres of pure water per kilogram of hydrogen—a consideration for arid regions with excellent solar resources.

### Safety and Public Perception

Hydrogen dissipates rapidly, is non-toxic, and produces no harmful combustion by-products. Nevertheless, stringent safety protocols and public education are essential.

## Conclusion: The Indispensable Piece of the Puzzle

Green hydrogen is not a silver bullet, but in hard-to-abate sectors it appears genuinely indispensable. The transition requires strategic deployment where hydrogen adds unique value, international cooperation on standards, and substantial infrastructure investment. The reward is a comprehensive decarbonisation strategy leaving no sector behind.
