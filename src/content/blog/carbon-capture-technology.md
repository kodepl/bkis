# Carbon Capture Technology: Engineering Solutions for a Warming Planet

Welcome to the BKIS Science Desk. As global temperatures rise and extreme weather events intensify, the mathematics of climate change have become starkly clear. Merely reducing future emissions is insufficient; we must also address the **legacy carbon dioxide** already accumulated in the atmosphere. Enter carbon capture technology—a suite of engineering approaches designed to trap, utilise, or permanently store CO₂. Some dismiss these technologies as excuses for continued fossil fuel use. Others see them as essential tools in a multi-pronged climate strategy. Today, we examine the science, the economics, and the controversies surrounding carbon capture.

## How Carbon Capture Works

Carbon capture technology spans two broad categories: **point-source capture**, which removes CO₂ from industrial emissions before they reach the atmosphere, and **direct air capture (DAC)**, which extracts CO₂ already dispersed in ambient air.

### Direct Air Capture Explained

Direct air capture facilities function like mechanical trees. Large fans draw air through contactors containing liquid solvents or solid sorbents that bind with CO₂ molecules. Heat is then applied to release the captured CO₂ in concentrated form, which can be stored or utilised. Leading companies in this space include **Climeworks** (Switzerland), **Carbon Engineering** (Canada), and **Global Thermostat** (United States).

The principal challenge is thermodynamic: CO₂ constitutes only **0.04% of atmospheric air**. Concentrating such a dilute gas requires substantial energy. For DAC to deliver genuine climate benefits, that energy must come from renewable or nuclear sources.

### Point-Source Capture

Point-source capture is technologically mature and already deployed at scale. Facilities attached to power plants, cement factories, and refineries capture CO₂ from concentrated exhaust streams using amine solvents or membrane separation. The **Boundary Dam project** in Saskatchewan, Canada, and **Petra Nova** in Texas demonstrated the feasibility of post-combustion capture on coal-fired power stations.

The cement and steel industries are particularly interested in point-source capture because these sectors are difficult to decarbonise through electrification alone.

## Storage and Utilisation

Captured carbon must go somewhere. Two pathways dominate: geological storage and utilisation.

### Geological Sequestration

The most permanent solution is **geological sequestration**—injecting compressed CO₂ deep underground into porous rock formations capped by impermeable layers. Depleted oil and gas fields and deep saline aquifers offer vast storage potential. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change estimates that global geological storage capacity exceeds **10,000 gigatonnes** of CO₂.

The Sleipner project in the North Sea has successfully stored over **16 million tonnes** of CO₂ beneath the seabed since 1996, providing a proof of concept for safe, long-term containment.

### Carbon Utilisation

Alternatively, captured CO₂ can become a feedstock for valuable products. **Carbon utilisation** applications include synthetic fuels, building materials (such as carbon-negative concrete), plastics, and chemicals. While these markets are promising, most utilisation pathways release CO₂ back into the atmosphere eventually, limiting their climate benefit compared to permanent storage.

## Leading Projects Worldwide

Around the globe, carbon capture projects are proliferating:

- **Orca (Iceland)**: Climeworks' facility captures 4,000 tonnes of CO₂ annually, powered entirely by geothermal energy.
- **Mammoth (Iceland)**: A scaled-up successor to Orca with capacity for 36,000 tonnes per year.
- **Northern Lights (Norway)**: A transport and storage network receiving CO₂ from industrial sites across Europe.
- **Drax Power Station (UK)**: Developing bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS), potentially generating negative emissions.

## Cost and Energy Challenges

The Achilles' heel of carbon capture is **cost**. Current estimates suggest direct air capture costs between **£200 and £600 per tonne** of CO₂ removed—far above the social cost of carbon or prevailing carbon prices. Point-source capture is cheaper, typically £40–£100 per tonne, but still adds significant expense to industrial operations.

Learning curves observed in solar and battery technologies suggest costs could fall substantially with deployment scale. Government subsidies, carbon pricing, and procurement mandates will be essential to bridge the gap.

## The Role of Policy and Incentives

Carbon capture does not exist in a vacuum; its viability depends on policy frameworks. The United States **Inflation Reduction Act** offers tax credits of up to $180 per tonne for direct air capture, catalysing massive investment. The UK government's **Power BECCS** and **Industrial Carbon Capture** business models aim to create revenue certainty for developers.

Critics argue that carbon capture funding diverts resources from renewables and energy efficiency. Proponents counter that some emissions are unavoidable, and legacy carbon removal is indispensable for meeting Paris Agreement targets.

## Conclusion

Carbon capture technology is neither a silver bullet nor a dangerous distraction. It is one component of a comprehensive climate response that must include emission reduction, ecosystem restoration, and societal adaptation. The technology works; the questions now concern scale, cost, and governance.
